ENG2003 Cover Letter

I chose this piece to reflect on my communication skills because it shows my ability to communicate professionally in a real-world context. Writing a cover letter for an actual mechanical engineering company taught me how personal branding and audience targeting can intersect, because it’s always easy to talk about yourself, but hard to do so strategically, and clearly. This assignment reflected major growth in tone and purpose, I realized how important it is to anticipate what the employer cares about, and to present myself not just as a student, but as a potential team member. It taught me how to use enthusiasm and professionalism together, which was slightly outside my comfort zone for writing as I have very limited experience with this type of writing. I think this is one of my best works, as it’s solid and written pretty well, and I’d like to keep refining the phrasing and add a few stronger examples of my experience, but overall, it represents a valuable skill in my professional communication toolkit.

ENG2003 Brochure

I selected this piece because it captures a complex global issue, antibiotic resistance, and breaks it down visually and clearly for both a general high-school level audience, as well as a highly educated technical audience, showcasing my development as a flexible communicator. I had to practice audience analysis in real time, what do teenagers know, and what could I tell them so that they can understand the dangers of AMR? How would researchers compare therapeutic strategies without overwhelming the readers? I used tiered vocabulary to target two audiences through two different brochures, one targeted at high school students that used lower-level terminology, and one that was a lot more technical, for the educated researchers who might be more interested in the details rather than the issue. Creating this piece forced me to synthesize dense microbiology content and completely reframe it for two different audiences with vastly different needs and literacy levels. It pushed me out of my comfort zone, because I was more familiar with long-form writing for only one audience at once, but this assignment made me think about the words I was using more, and the way I presented information to adapt to the audience better. I think it is some of my best work, and I’m proud of the clarity and purpose of each section, however I would improve the layout and design further, but overall the communication is strong, effective, and tailored, which is a skill I would continue to develop as I go through my career.

ENG2003 Term Project 1 Report

For the ENG2003 course, I wrote a technical report that covered a dual approach to combating multidrug-resistant bacteria, or MDRs. I decided to pick it because it was some of the most demanding work I completed academically ever, as it pushed me to develop my skills in writing effectively, and communicate a complex topic for a non-technical general audience. The report covered the two methods, bacteriophage therapy, and artificial intelligence-driven antibiotic discovery, while integrating peer-reviewed sources to maintain accuracy throughout all my statements. I practiced audience adaptation by applying the 7 C’s and revising sections multiple times for clarity, while still preserving the scientific integrity. The paper demonstrates my ability to research using online resources, validate their credibility, and then use the sources effectively to communicate clearly in a structured technical report, showcasing my growth and progress as a writer, and marks a major step in my writing and communication journey.

Reflection

Part 1: Experience with ENG2003

During my time in ENG2003, I had a surprisingly rewarding experience, even though I wasn’t expecting communication and writing to have such a major role in the engineering field. At first, I thought this course was more about grammar and formatting, but since Microsoft word and Google docs can all do that for us now, it focused on way more strategic and creative methods, which I thought was great. The assignments pushed me to think like a designer, not just a writer, and helped me develop skills that would actually help me in the real-world, rather than doing things that would be more fit for a high school English class. I found that the most useful portions were the group projects and group work, because they encouraged us to develop effective communication skills which we would be able to transfer to the workplace. The 7 C’s; clarity, conciseness, concreteness, correctness, coherence, completeness, and courtesy, were particularly useful in guiding my approach to writing, and they encourage good writing practices to help me convey my ideas in the most effective ways. Understanding this helped me become more aware of the impact that better communication skills would have while working with others, both verbally and linguistically, but also non-verbally and subconsciously. Learning how to create effective technical reports and presentations will be a skill that I will use throughout the rest of my career, so having a good foundation and understanding of it from ENG2003 will ensure that my reports and presentations will be effective and leave a lasting impact on the audience.

Part 2: My growth and development as a communicator

When I started ENG2003, I was still starting to hone my skills as a communicator, I had the spelling and grammar down from the start, but I wasn’t the most confident speaker in presentations, my communication skills were way better through text or email, or any other written formats. My speaking and presentation skills improved the most throughout ENG2003, especially after learning the STAR method, which helps me cover the most important things; the situation at hand, the task I was given, the action that I did, and the result I got, which helped me stop myself from going on and on while speaking without the audience understanding anything. The 7 C’s remind me to be concise, even while speaking, so using filler words and filler sounds just adds time and words to a presentation or speech, while adding no useful information for the audience, creating word fatigue. One thing I’m still working on while speaking is the pacing, as sometimes I can go too fast, causing the audience to lose track of what I’m talking about, or even losing track of my original point myself, so I’m working on slowing down my pace to sound more confident, while also keeping the audience more engaged with the main idea.
